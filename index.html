Events

The major events that occurred in the Beginnings time period (click on each dropdown)

  • First Vision

    “I saw a pillar of light exactly over my head, above the brightness of the sun, which descended gradually until it fell upon me. … When the light rested upon me I saw two Personages, whose brightness and glory defy all description, standing above me in the air. One of them spake unto me, calling me by name and said, pointing to the other—This is My Beloved Son. Hear Him!” (Joseph Smith—History 1:16–17).

    There were four primary accounts:
    1832: Focuses on personal redemptioin
    1835: Focuses most on visionary, otherworldly experience
    1838: Focuses most on the which church to join, with pre and post vision details
    1842: Focuses most on a concise summary for non-LDS audience

    There were two primary doctrinal messages from the First Vision:
    1. Doctrinal apostasy through creeds
    2. Apostasy from authorized covenants/ordinances

  • Translation of the Book of Mormon

    Moroni first visited Joseph Smith in 1823. He finally obained the plates in 1827 and completed the translation by 1829.

    Joseph translated the Book of Mormon with two tools:
    He used “interpreters”, also called “Urim and Thummim”, which were two clear stones placed in a metal rim that one could look through.
    The other was a seer stone that he put in a hat to block out the light and then placed his face in the hat to look through.

    "Three witnesses (Oliver Cowdery, David Whitmer, and Martin Harris) declared an angel of God appeared to them and showed them the Book of Mormon plates and they heard the voice of the Lord pronounce that Joseph Smith’s translation had been accomplished “by the gift and power of God.”" - "Witnesses of the Book of Mormon"

    "An additional eight witnesses (members of the Smith and Whitmer families)1 declared that Joseph Smith himself showed them the plates and allowed each to “heft” the ancient artifact and examine its engravings." - "Witnesses of the Book of Mormon"

  • Organization of the Church

    John the Baptist restored the Aaronic priesthood and the keys of the ministering of angels, the gospel of repentence, and baptism by immersion.
    Peter, James, and John restored the Melchizedek priesthood and the keys of apostleship, the kingdom, and the dispensation of the fulness of times.
    The Church was organized on April 6, 1830 in Fayette, New York in Peter Whitmer Sr’s house.

Events

The major events that occurred in the Two Church Centers time period (click on each dropdown)

  • Book of Commandments

    The Book of Commandments (now known as the Doctrine and Covenants) was the original title of Joseph’s printed book of revelations.

    The original had:
    a. Theology on the Doctrine of the Church. This was the lectures on faith given by Sidney Rigdon
    b. Covenants and Commandments of the Lord. These were Joseph’s revelations

    There were various origins of how revelations were received:
    The voice of the Lord, Urim and Thummim, visions, letters, and dinner table discussions

    The Doctrine and Covenants is the book of scripture that is the foundation of the Lord's church

  • Joseph Smith Translation

    Joseph Smith interpreted the Bible. He clarified existing passages, restored lost doctrine, added new information, and applied the doctrine.

    He made over three thousand changes to the Bible and about one-third of those changes are now in the footnotes of the Bible.

  • The Book of Abraham

    The Book of Abraham came from papyrus and mummies that were excavated then bought by Michael Chandler. They are writings of Abraham while he was in Egypt. It was canonized in Pearl of Great Price in 1880. Emma Smith sold the papyrus and mummies and then they were burned in the Chicago fire. The remaining fragments then got to the Church later.

    The Abrahamic Covenant described using three P's:
    1. Promised land (celestial kingdom)
    2. Priesthood (authority, power, ordinances)
    3. Posterity (eternal marriage and increase)

    There are validity challenges with the Book of Abraham today because none of the characters on the remaining papyri mention things of the book of Abraham

The major events that occurred in the Nauvoo time period (click on each dropdown)

  • Eternal and Plural Marriage

    The “new and everlasting covenant of marriage” is a man and a woman being sealed for eternity by the power of the Holy Spirit.
    Eternal marriage is an ordiance required for exaltation and to become like God.

    According to Doctrine and Covenants 132, there are three reasons why the Church practiced plural marriage:
    1. The Lord commanded it
    2. It was part of Joseph’s appointment to restore all things
    3. To multiply and replenish the earth, according to God’s commandment, or to raise up seed unto God as the Book of Mormon states

    "Joseph Smith entered a plural marriage in the 1830s, though it did not last. Then, between early 1841 and the fall of 1843, Joseph was sealed to approximately thirty women." - Steven C. Harper (pg. 481)

  • Martyrdom of Joseph Smith

    "Dissenters from the Church published the one and only issue of an opposition newspaper they called the Nauvoo Expositor. The publishers were a former First Counselor in the First Presidency, William Law; Law’s brother, Wilson; Charles Ivins; Charles and Robert Foster; and Francis and Chauncey Higbee.1 The dissenters, several of whom had been recently excommunicated, published the Expositor to stir up controversy over practices and teachings with which they strongly disagreed. Using inflammatory language, they voiced their discontent with the practice of plural marriage, Joseph Smith’s teachings on the nature of God from his recent King Follett sermon, and his mixing of religious and civic authority in Nauvoo." - Nauvoo Expositor (article)

    Joseph ordered a marshal to destroy the printing press for the Nauvoo Expositor to prevent the incorrect news spreading and a potential riot. Joseph was then arrested on charges for destroying the printing press and inciting a riot.

    While Joseph and Hyrum Smith, William Richards, and John Taylor were in a room of Carthage jail, a mob attacked, killing Hyrum then Joseph.

    “…and at the same time confirming my worst fears, that Joseph was assuredly dead. I felt a dull lonely sickening sensation at the news. When I reflected that our noble chieftain the Prophet of the living God had fallen, and that I had seen his brother in the cold embrace of death, it seemed as though there was an open void or vacuum in the great field of human existence to me, and a dark gloomy chasm, blank or, void in the Kingdom and that we were left alone. Oh! how lonely was that feeling! How cold, barren and desolate! In the midst of difficulties he was always the first in motion; in critical positions his counsel was always sought.” - John Taylor

  • Succession

    There was a debate on who would succeed now that Joseph died. The succession debate of 1844 included Brigham Young and Sidney Rigdon. Brigham Young was the senior Apotle and President of the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles. Sidney Rigdon was the First Counselor in the First Presidency and asked to be sustained as the "Guardian" of the Church.

    Though the Prophet Joseph Smith was dead, the Apostles held all the Priesthood keys needed to preside over the Church. “The Twelve are not subject to any other than the first presidency, … and where I am not, there is no first presidency; over the twelve” - Joseph Smith. Therefore, there was no First Presidency over the Twelve after Joseph's death. Brigham Young became the next prophet. We still follow this same process today; the senior Apostle becomes the next prophet.

The major events that occurred in the Church in the West time period (click on each dropdown)

  • Movement to Utah

    "On April 16, 1847, a group of 148 Latter-day Saints in 72 wagons left Nebraska and began the 1,031-mile journey to the Salt Lake Valley. They arrived in the Salt Lake Valley between the 21st and 24th of July 1847. In the years that followed, tens of thousands of Latter-day Saints from around the world would leave their homes and follow the same route to the Salt Lake Valley. Today, Latter-day Saints everywhere continue this pioneering legacy in their own countries as the gospel of Jesus Christ spreads throughout the world." - Pioneers 1847–Today

    Several groups of members traveled long distances and suffered greatly so that we could be here in Utah today.

  • End of Plural Marriage

    "The Bible and the Book of Mormon teach that monogamy is God’s standard for marriage unless He declares otherwise (see 2 Samuel 12:7–8 and Jacob 2:27, 30). Following a revelation to Joseph Smith, the practice of plural marriage was instituted among Church members in the early 1840s (see section 132). From the 1860s to the 1880s, the United States government passed laws to make this religious practice illegal. These laws were eventually upheld by the U.S. Supreme Court. After receiving revelation, President Wilford Woodruff issued the following Manifesto, which was accepted by the Church as authoritative and binding on October 6, 1890. This led to the end of the practice of plural marriage in the Church." - Official Declaration 1

  • Endowments for the Dead

    The first endowments for the dead happened in the St. George, Utah temple on January 11, 1877

    "Endowments commenced in the [St. George] Temple and for the first time Endowments for the Dead in this Dispensation. 72 persons received their Endowments. I took the lead in the washing and anointing and instructions in the same. Washed, anointed and clothed the first person and took the general lead of the same, all through by promptings by the direction of President Brigham Young through Elder Woodruff. We were late getting through. It was the most responsible and complicated day’s work I [have] ever done, as most of the workmen were new in the labor and the prompting devolved almost entirely on me for nearly all the parts." - Alonzo H. Raleigh

Events

The major events that occurred in the Expansion time period (click on each dropdown)

  • Official Declaration 2

    Official Declaration 2 allowed all worthy men to hold the priesthood.

    "The Book of Mormon teaches that “all are alike unto God,” including “black and white, bond and free, male and female” (2 Nephi 26:33). Throughout the history of the Church, people of every race and ethnicity in many countries have been baptized and have lived as faithful members of the Church. During Joseph Smith’s lifetime, a few black male members of the Church were ordained to the priesthood. Early in its history, Church leaders stopped conferring the priesthood on black males of African descent. Church records offer no clear insights into the origins of this practice. Church leaders believed that a revelation from God was needed to alter this practice and prayerfully sought guidance. The revelation came to Church President Spencer W. Kimball and was affirmed to other Church leaders in the Salt Lake Temple on June 1, 1978. The revelation removed all restrictions with regard to race that once applied to the priesthood." - Official Declaration 2

  • First Missionary Lessons

    The first formal missionary lessons published by the Church were called "A Systematic Program for Teaching the Gospel". They were published in 1952, with Gordon B. Hinckley as a main contributor. In this edition, "missionaries taught seven lessons ― through a logical presentation of gospel principles, fortified by scripture, together with reading, convincing testimony, and sincere prayer." - "A Historical Analysis of How Preach My Gospel Came to Be"

    Other editions were published. The lessons became discussions in "A Uniform System for Teaching Investigators" in 1961. The discussions were to be completely memorized.

    Then came "The Uniform System for Teaching Families" in 1973 and "Uniform System for Teaching the Gospel" in 1986.

    "Preach My Gospel" was published in 2004
